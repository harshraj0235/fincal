Tomorrow Market Crash or Rally? Exact Levels to Watch

By MoneyCal Team • 4 जुलाई 2026

Traders, buckle up. The final Tuesday of June 2026 is shaping up to be one of the most volatile trading sessions of the quarter. Thanks to the revised NSE structure, we are facing a massive "triple expiry"—Nifty weekly, Nifty monthly, and Bank Nifty monthly contracts are all expiring on the exact same day.

Why Expiry Days Cause Chaos

On expiry days, institutional investors and large hedge funds are forced to either close their derivative positions or roll them over to the next month (the July series). This massive reshuffling of capital creates extreme price swings, especially in the final two hours of trading.

Furthermore, options writers (the "smart money") actively try to pin the market to the "Max Pain" point—the specific strike price where the maximum number of options contracts expire worthless, allowing them to pocket the premiums.

Critical Levels to Watch: Nifty 50

For the broader market, all eyes are on the psychological 24,000 mark.

  • Bullish Scenario: If Nifty sustains above 24,000 in the first half, short sellers will be forced to cover their positions, potentially triggering a massive "short covering rally" toward 24,200.
  • Bearish Scenario: If the 23,800 support level breaks, panic selling could drag the index down sharply as long positions are forcibly liquidated.

Bank Nifty: The Wildcard

Bank Nifty is historically more volatile than Nifty, and this expiry is no different. With recent macroeconomic news affecting banking stocks, traders are closely monitoring the 57,600 to 58,500 zone.

  • Support: Strong put writing is visible at the 57,500 level. Breaking below this could lead to a rapid 300-400 point drop.
  • Resistance: The 58,500 level is acting as a massive wall. Only a decisive breakout above this zone will invite fresh buying momentum.

A Warning for Retail Traders

While expiry days offer the temptation of "zero-to-hero" trades (where cheap out-of-the-money options suddenly spike in value), they are incredibly dangerous. The implied volatility (IV) crush can wipe out your capital in minutes even if you get the market direction right. Consider reducing your position sizing and using strict stop-losses.
